VOICE OF CHILD REPORTS: What is a Voice of Child Report? A Voice of Child Report is one way for children to be heard in a family law proceedings. It is a written report that is prepared by a professional who has training and expertise working with children, such as a social worker, counselor psychologist, and who is very familiar with the family court process. The report gives information about the child’s views and preferences and puts this into context. Reports of this nature are often ordered by a judge. When would a Voice of Child Report be appropriate? A Voice of Child Report is typically appropriate for children who are over the age of ten (10), although in certain situations it can be appropriate for younger children. Voice of Child Reports can be particularly useful in a family court proceeding where the parents have opposing views about what is in the best interests of their child and they have been unable to come to resolution. Why is a Voice of Child Report Important? The “best interest of the child” is what guides judges when they make decisions in family court. To assist in figuring out what is in a child’s best interests, judges may need to learn about and understand the points of the child and also information that the child may have, such as parenting information. The report may also assist the parties, lawyers and judges lean about and better understand the child’s perspective. Information contained in the report may be used to help those involved make decisions about parenting arrangements What is the process of a Voice of Child Report? A voice of child report can typically be prepared within thirty (30) days and usually involves between ten (10) to fifteen (15) hours of work. The involved professional will usually meet privately with the child on two (2) or three (3) occasions. The involved professional may also wish to speak with a third party, such as the child’s teacher. Although the parents have minimal involvement they are typically made aware of the process and will need to give written consent for the involved professional to proceed. PARENTING MEDIATION: What Is Parenting Mediation? Parenting mediation is a structured, collaborative process where a mediator supports parents to negotiate post-separation parenting arrangements through mutual agreement. Why Attend Parenting Mediation? Because parenting is a long-term commitment it requires ongoing collaboration and effective communication between separated parents over many years. Parents who are able to reach agreement with regard to their respective roles and responsibilities (as separated parents) can provide a healthy family environment for their children. Parenting mediation provides a collaborative forum for parents to discuss and develop a Parenting Plan with a focus on the needs of the children. In addition, by participating in the mediation process parents can build skills for working together in the future and develop methods for resolving any parenting challenges that may arise. Who are Parenting Mediators? Parenting mediators are usually professionals who have an educational background and experience of working with families, such as: social workers, counsellors and psychologists, and have specialty training in Family Mediation. Family mediations specialise in mediating parenting issues, including situations involving high conflict. Clients who wish to additionally mediate their financial issues are referred to lawyer-mediators who specialize in mediating property and support issues. What Is The Role Of The Parenting Mediator? A mediator’s role is to facilitate the discussions between the parents so that each has an opportunity to express his/her point of view. The mediator may clarify concerns and misunderstandings and assist parents to consider different options and give priority to the needs of the children. Parenting mediators may also provide information about children’s developmental and social needs in the context of separation. Mediators support parents to make decisions and come to mutual agreement. Mediators do not provide counselling services or offer legal advice. Mediators do not testify in court. PARENTING CONSULTATON: What Is Parenting Consultation? Parenting consultation is where a trained professional, such as social worker, counsellor or psychologist, provides information and guidance surrounding children’s needs in separation and may include: preparing a child for the separation, considering child development factors when making a Parenting Plan, developing strategies for working collaboratively with the other parent. You may also be referred to other parenting resources that may be available in the community. When To Seek Parenting Consultation? Parents can seek Parenting Consultation at any time during the separation or post-separation. If parents’ have concerns regarding their children’s adjustment or in regard to the co-parenting relationship it may be appropriate to seek this services. Parents may self-refer or may be referred by lawyers, counsellors or other professionals. Parenting Consultation is most useful when both parents participate; however, one parent alone may seek consultation. What Is Involved In Parenting Consultation? After obtaining initial information the Parenting Consultant can usually determine what steps are required to assist the family. It may involve only one meeting with one or both parents; or it may involve several meetings. Parenting Consultation is typically short-term. What Is The Role Of The Parenting Consultant? The Consultant’s role is to assist parents to manage their separation process in a manner that minimizes stress to the children and helps the parents work together effectively as separated parents. Parenting Consultation is not an assessment. The Consultant does not provide any report or recommendations for court purposes. PRIVACY CONCERNS: WHAT ARE MY PRIVACY RIGHTS?
